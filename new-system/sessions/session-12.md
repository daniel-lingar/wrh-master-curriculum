# Session 12 — Internal Behavioral Conflict Systems

## Plain-Language Teaching Title: Two Survival Systems Can Fight Inside One Person

## Facilitator Notes — NOT READ ALOUD

**Purpose:** Teach participants that internal contradiction is often not deception, weakness, bad character, or lack of desire. A person can honestly want two opposite things at the same time because different survival systems are trying to protect them from different threats. This session explains internal conflict as competing protective strategies, not proof that someone is fake, hopeless, or two-faced.

**Risk Rating:** Moderate to High-Yellow. This session may activate shame around relapse, relationships, court compliance, parenting, work, treatment, avoidance, anger, numbness, and self-sabotage. Participants may recognize that different parts of them have been pulling in different directions for years.

**Key Anchors to Maintain:**

- This session does not diagnose dissociative identity disorder or any other condition.
- Use plain language: parts, sides, systems, pulls, alarms, protectors.
- Do not encourage participants to split themselves into identities.
- The goal is observation and choice, not labeling.
- No participant has to disclose their inner conflict.
- Internal conflict does not erase responsibility. It explains why responsibility can feel blocked or unstable.

**Materials:** Whiteboard for the Internal Conflict Loop. Optional participant handout: The Parts Check.

**Delivery Tone:** Steady, normalizing, non-dramatic. Many participants may have been called manipulative, inconsistent, lazy, unstable, or self-sabotaging. Reduce shame while preserving accountability.

---

## Session Purpose — NOT READ ALOUD

Teach participants that contradictory behavior can come from competing survival systems inside one person. One system may want safety. Another may want relief. Another may want connection. Another may want escape. This session shifts the question from “Why am I lying to myself?” to “What are the different protective systems inside me trying to prevent?”

**Participant Takeaway:**  
A part of me can want one thing while another part wants the opposite. That does not mean I am fake or hopeless. It means different survival systems are trying to protect me. I can learn to notice the conflict, identify what each side is protecting, and choose the next step more consciously.

## Core Teaching Section One — READ ALOUD

“Let’s define internal conflict in plain language.

Internal conflict is when different protective systems inside a person pull in different directions.

One side may want safety.

One side may want relief.

One side may want connection.

One side may want control.

One side may want distance.

One side may want justice.

One side may want silence.

One side may want to repair.

One side may want to avoid shame at all costs.

These are not always logical.

They are protective.

The nervous system does not organize itself around what looks best on paper.

It organizes itself around what once helped the person survive.

So if telling the truth once led to punishment, one part may still resist truth.

If closeness once led to pain, one part may resist closeness.

If sobriety means feeling grief with no buffer, one part may resist sobriety.

If showing up to court means facing shame, authority, and possible punishment, one part may resist showing up.

If work is the only place a person feels valuable, one part may resist rest.

From the outside, this can look inconsistent.

From the inside, it feels like a fight.

I want this.

I do not want this.

I care.

I cannot move.

I love them.

I avoid them.

I want help.

I do not trust help.

I want to change.

I want the pain to stop right now.

That is not simple hypocrisy.

It is often survival systems colliding.”

## Core Teaching Section Two — READ ALOUD

“Let’s name common internal conflicts.

**First: connection versus protection.**

One part wants to be close. Another part says closeness leads to danger, rejection, control, or abandonment. The person may reach out, then disappear. They may ask for love, then push it away.

**Second: responsibility versus shame avoidance.**

One part wants to handle the bill, the court date, the apology, the phone call, the paperwork, the treatment plan. Another part says facing it will be unbearable. The person may care deeply and still avoid.

**Third: sobriety versus relief.**

One part wants recovery, stability, family, health, and freedom. Another part wants immediate relief from grief, panic, shame, boredom, exhaustion, or body pain. The person may mean it when they say they want to stop, and still reach for relief when the system overloads.

**Fourth: honesty versus safety.**

One part wants to tell the truth. Another part says the truth will lead to punishment, rejection, humiliation, or loss. The person may hide, minimize, delay, or go silent.

**Fifth: rest versus worth.**

One part knows the body is exhausted. Another part says rest is laziness, danger, or loss of value. The person keeps working until the body forces a stop.

Here is the rule:

Internal conflict is not proof that nothing is real.

It may mean too many things are real at once.

The love is real.

The fear is real.

The desire to change is real.

The desire for relief is real.

The accountability is real.

The avoidance is real.

The person is not always lying when they contradict themselves.

They may be reporting from whichever survival system is loudest in that moment.”

## Lived-Pattern Anchor from From the Storm to the Fire — READ ALOUD

“Let’s look at how this worked in the Architect’s life.

Daniel had more than one system running inside him.

One part of him wanted to be a good father.

That was real.

One part of him wanted to explain everything to his children, repair what broke, and be understood.

That was real.

Another part froze around the very contact he wanted.

Another part feared rejection so much that reaching out felt like touching a live wire.

Another part carried shame so heavy that silence sometimes felt safer than trying.

All of those were real at the same time.

That is internal conflict.

In addiction, one part wanted to stop.

One part knew meth was destroying him.

One part wanted peace, health, work, family, and stability.

Another part wanted relief now.

Relief from grief.

Relief from shame.

Relief from freeze.

Relief from the body that would not move.

Relief from being alone with his own mind.

Those systems fought inside one person.

That does not make the harm harmless.

It does not make relapse meaningless.

It does not erase responsibility.

But it changes the question.

Not:

Why did he lie about wanting to change?

But:

Which system took over when the pain got too loud?

In court and paperwork situations, one part knew the consequence.

One part wanted to comply.

One part wanted to be done with the fear.

Another part treated the system as danger.

Another part froze.

Another part avoided because avoidance gave temporary relief.

Then shame came in and called him irresponsible, broken, hopeless, or bad.

That shame became another system inside him.

One that attacked him from the inside before anyone else could.

This is how a person becomes divided without being fake.

The person is not one clean line.

The person is a nervous system full of old strategies trying to protect against different threats.

WRH does not ask:

Which part is the real you?

WRH asks:

Which system is driving right now, what is it protecting, and what choice can you make from the whole self instead of only the loudest alarm?”

## Mechanism Map — READ ALOUD

*(Facilitator — draw this on the board or speak clearly.)*

“Let’s map the Internal Conflict Loop.

**Step 1 — Present Demand or Trigger**

A situation appears: a relationship, court date, craving, apology, bill, job demand, child contact, treatment requirement, rest, conflict, or decision.

**Step 2 — Multiple Threats Are Detected**

Different systems detect different dangers.

Abandonment.

Punishment.

Shame.

Failure.

Pain.

Loss of control.

Exposure.

Loneliness.

Withdrawal.

Rejection.

**Step 3 — Competing Protective Strategies Activate**

One system says move toward.

One says run.

One says freeze.

One says please.

One says use.

One says hide.

One says confess.

One says deny.

One says work harder.

One says disappear.

**Step 4 — Behavior Becomes Inconsistent**

The person makes a promise and breaks it.

Reaches out and pulls back.

Shows up once and disappears later.

Says they care but acts avoidant.

Wants recovery but seeks relief.

Plans to comply but freezes.

**Step 5 — Misreading**

Others may call the person manipulative, lazy, fake, unstable, two-faced, dramatic, or not serious.

The person may believe those labels.

**Step 6 — Shame Intensifies the Conflict**

Shame attacks the person internally.

The system becomes more threatened.

Different parts fight harder.

The person loses access to clear choice.

**Step 7 — Interruption Point**

Name the conflict without shame.

A part of me wants this.

Another part wants that.

Both are trying to protect me from something.

Now I need to choose from the whole picture, not just the loudest alarm.

That is the interruption.

Not instant healing.

Not perfect behavior.

A pause where choice becomes more possible.”

## Core Teaching Section Three — READ ALOUD

“Let’s be careful with language.

When WRH says ‘part of me,’ we are not diagnosing anyone.

We are using ordinary human language.

People say this all the time.

Part of me wants to go.

Part of me wants to stay.

Part of me knows better.

Part of me does not care anymore.

Part of me wants to forgive.

Part of me is still angry.

Part of me wants help.

Part of me does not trust anyone.

That language is not strange.

It is honest.

Human beings are complex.

Trauma and survival make that complexity louder.

The goal is not to split yourself into pieces.

The goal is to stop letting one survival state pretend to be the whole truth.

If anger is loud, anger may say:

I am the truth.

If shame is loud, shame may say:

I am the truth.

If craving is loud, craving may say:

I am the truth.

If freeze is loud, freeze may say:

Nothing is possible.

But WRH teaches:

A loud system is not always the whole self.

The whole self is the one who can notice the systems.

That is where choice begins.”

## Practical Tool / Reframe — READ ALOUD

“Here is the tool for this week.

It is called the Parts Check.

Use it when you feel torn, confused, self-sabotaging, or pulled in opposite directions.

**Step one: Name the pulls.**

Silently say:

A part of me wants ______.

Another part of me wants ______.

No judgment.

Just name the pulls.

**Step two: Ask what each side is protecting.**

For each side, ask:

What are you trying to protect me from?

What are you afraid would happen if you did not do your job?

**Step three: Choose from the whole picture.**

Ask:

What choice honors both concerns as much as possible?

If I cannot honor both, whose concern is most urgent right now?

Then make one choice.

Not because the other side is stupid.

Not because the other side is bad.

Because you are the one who has to live with the consequences.

The goal is not to silence every inner conflict.

The goal is to stop being dragged by whichever system screams the loudest.”

## Implementation Notes — NOT READ ALOUD

**For Facilitators:**

- Do not diagnose participants or imply dissociative identity disorder. This session uses ordinary language about internal conflict.
- If a participant becomes concerned that they are “crazy” because they recognize different parts, normalize gently: “Most people experience inner conflict. We are studying protective pulls, not diagnosing identities.”
- Do not let the group use this session to excuse harm. Use: “Understanding the competing systems does not erase impact. It helps create a better choice point.”
- Do not ask participants to share their conflicts aloud.
- The Parts Check is private.
- This session may be especially important for addiction recovery, reentry, family restoration, treatment compliance, and parenting contexts.
- If someone is in active crisis, suicidal, at risk of harm, or severely dissociated, follow host-site crisis protocols.
- This session bridges into dissociation because unresolved internal conflict can become shutdown, numbness, fog, or distance from the moment.
- WRH is education, not treatment, diagnosis, medical advice, legal advice, or crisis care.
