# Session 13 — Dissociative Protective Disengagement States

## Plain-Language Teaching Title: Leaving Your Body Was the Only Exit Left

## Facilitator Notes — NOT READ ALOUD

**Purpose:** Teach participants that dissociation is a protective disengagement state, not craziness, weakness, lying, laziness, or lack of caring. This session explains how the mind can create distance from the present moment when the body cannot escape what feels overwhelming.

**Risk Rating:** High-Yellow. This session may activate participants with histories of abuse, violence, grief, panic, addiction, shutdown, depersonalization, derealization, memory gaps, or “behind glass” experiences. Keep grounding slow, concrete, and optional.

**Key Anchors to Maintain:**

- Dissociation is protection through distance.
- Numbness does not mean the person does not care.
- Memory gaps do not automatically mean dishonesty.
- The goal is not to force full presence all at once.
- The goal is safe return, one sensation at a time.
- No participant has to describe what made them dissociate.
- This session does not diagnose dissociative identity disorder.

**Materials:** Whiteboard or slide for the Dissociation Distance Loop. Optional participant handout: The Grounding Check.

**Delivery Tone:** Calm, slow, normalizing, careful. Do not dramatize dissociation. Do not make participants feel strange or defective for recognizing themselves.

---

## Session Purpose — NOT READ ALOUD

Teach participants that dissociation is the nervous system creating distance from a moment that feels too much to fully experience. It may show up as numbness, fog, time loss, blankness, feeling unreal, watching oneself from outside, or being “behind glass.” This session shifts the question from “Why am I so disconnected?” to “What was my mind protecting me from feeling all at once?”

**Participant Takeaway:**  
Dissociation helped me survive moments where staying fully present felt impossible. I do not have to hate that protection. I can learn to come back slowly, safely, one sensation at a time.

## Core Teaching Section One — READ ALOUD

“Let’s define dissociation in plain language.

Dissociation is the nervous system creating distance from the present moment.

It can be emotional distance.

Physical distance.

Memory distance.

Reality distance.

Identity distance.

It can feel like fog.

It can feel like numbness.

It can feel like watching from far away.

It can feel like your body is in the room but you are not fully there.

Why would the mind do that?

Because sometimes full presence feels unbearable.

If a child cannot leave a violent room, the mind may leave internally.

If a person cannot stop abuse, the mind may go far away.

If grief is too large, the system may go numb.

If shame is too intense, the brain may blank.

If panic is too strong, the world may feel unreal.

If the institution, relationship, memory, or body sensation feels like too much, the mind may create distance.

That does not mean the person is making it up.

It does not mean they are weak.

It does not mean they do not care.

It means the system found a way to lower the intensity.

Dissociation can be protective.

But protection can become a problem when it turns on in moments where presence is needed.

A person may dissociate during a conversation with their child.

During a court hearing.

During therapy.

During intimacy.

During conflict.

During work.

During grief.

During recovery.

During a medical appointment.

They may look present from the outside, but inside they are far away.

That is why dissociation is often misunderstood.”

## Core Teaching Section Two — READ ALOUD

“Let’s name some forms dissociation can take.

**First: emotional numbness.**

This is when something painful happens, but the feeling does not come. You know it should hurt. You know it matters. But inside, there is flatness. Numbness is not proof that you do not care. It may be the system putting distance between you and pain.

**Second: going blank.**

Someone asks a question. Conflict rises. A decision needs to be made. Suddenly, the mind is empty. Words disappear. Memory disappears. You may know later what you wanted to say, but in the moment there is nothing.

**Third: derealization.**

The world feels unreal, foggy, dreamlike, distant, or strange. The room may look slightly wrong. Sounds may feel far away. You may feel like you are present but not fully inside the moment.

**Fourth: depersonalization.**

You feel disconnected from yourself. Like you are watching yourself from outside. Like your body is doing things and you are observing. Like your own voice or hands do not feel fully yours.

**Fifth: time loss or memory gaps.**

You lose track of time. You do not remember parts of a conversation. You arrive somewhere and do not remember the trip. You may feel ashamed because others assume you were not paying attention.

Here is the important part:

Dissociation is not always dramatic.

Sometimes it is quiet.

A person sitting still.

A person saying, ‘I’m fine.’

A person looking calm.

A person doing the task but not fully being there.

A person smiling while far away inside.

This is why the WRH rule matters:

Do not confuse appearance with internal state.

A quiet person may not be calm.

A numb person may not be healed.

A blank person may not be refusing.

A disconnected person may not be uncaring.

They may be protected by distance.”

## Lived-Pattern Anchor from From the Storm to the Fire — READ ALOUD

“Let’s look at how this worked in the Architect’s life.

Daniel had moments where the only available exit was internal.

The glass box taught distance before language.

The blanket taught freeze.

The body signaling taught him that the body could speak before the mind understood.

But when the danger, grief, shame, or pressure became too much, freeze was not the only response.

Sometimes he went behind the glass inside himself.

That phrase matters.

Behind the glass.

Present enough to be seen.

Far enough away to survive.

As a child, when violence filled the room, the mind could create distance from the terror.

Later, when grief stacked loss on loss, the same distance showed up as numbness.

People died.

Funerals happened.

Calls came.

Names were added to the list.

And sometimes the system could not feel each loss cleanly.

It went flat.

Not because love was absent.

Because the grief was too much to hold all at once.

When his mother died, freeze and dissociation lived close together.

He found her collapsed.

His body locked.

Later, at the funeral, he could not walk all the way to the casket.

A person outside the system might judge that.

But inside, the body and mind were trying to survive contact with a grief that was too large.

In court, in conflict, in relationships, in phone calls, in shame, the same pattern could appear.

Blankness.

Avoidance.

Fog.

Distance.

A mind that could not stay fully present because full presence felt like collapse.

That does not erase harm.

Dissociation can hurt relationships.

It can make people feel abandoned.

It can make communication fail.

It can make responsibility harder.

But naming dissociation gives a better starting point than shame.

Instead of saying:

I am cold.

I do not care.

I am broken.

WRH asks:

What was too much to feel in that moment?

Where did my mind go?

And what would help me come back safely?”

## Mechanism Map — READ ALOUD

*(Facilitator — draw this on the board or speak clearly.)*

“Let’s map the Dissociation Distance Loop.

**Step 1 — Overwhelming Input**

The person experiences threat, shame, grief, pain, conflict, sensory overload, fear, authority pressure, relationship intensity, or emotional intensity that feels too much for the system.

**Step 2 — No Safe External Exit**

The body cannot leave, fight, fix, speak, or solve the moment. The person may feel trapped physically, emotionally, socially, legally, or relationally.

**Step 3 — Internal Exit**

The mind creates distance. Fog. numbness. blankness. watching from outside. time loss. feeling unreal. feeling disconnected.

**Step 4 — Short-Term Protection**

The distance lowers the intensity. The person survives the moment without feeling all of it at once.

**Step 5 — Present-Life Cost**

The person may lose connection, memory, presence, communication, follow-through, emotional availability, or access to choice.

**Step 6 — Misreading**

Others may call it coldness, dishonesty, laziness, avoidance, lack of care, disrespect, not listening, or noncompliance.

**Step 7 — Shame and More Disconnection**

The person feels defective or guilty. Shame becomes more overwhelming input. The system dissociates more.

**Step 8 — Interruption Point**

Someone names the mechanism:

This is dissociation.

My mind is creating distance.

I do not have to force myself fully back all at once.

I can return through one sensation, one object, one sound, one breath, one safe connection.

That is the interruption.

Not force.

Return.”

## Core Teaching Section Three — READ ALOUD

“Let’s be careful here.

Some people hear the word dissociation and get scared.

They think it means they are crazy.

They think it means they are dangerous.

They think it means they are lying.

They think it means they are not really themselves.

That is not what we are saying.

We are saying the nervous system can create distance when presence feels impossible.

That distance exists on a spectrum.

Most people have mild versions of it.

Driving on autopilot.

Losing track of a conversation.

Staring into space.

Going blank under pressure.

Feeling numb during grief.

The more overwhelming the history or the present demand, the stronger the distance can become.

If dissociation is severe, dangerous, or causing major memory gaps, a person deserves clinical support.

But recognizing distance does not mean you are defective.

It means your system found a way to survive.

WRH does not shame the exit.

WRH teaches return.”

## Practical Tool / Reframe — READ ALOUD

“Here is your tool for this week.

It is called the Grounding Check.

Use it when you feel foggy, far away, numb, unreal, or disconnected.

It has three steps.

**Step one: Name it.**

Silently say:

I am dissociating.

My mind is creating distance because something feels like too much.

This is protection.

I can come back slowly.

If the word dissociation feels too big, use simpler words:

I am far away right now.

My system is creating distance.

I can return slowly.

**Step two: Anchor to the body.**

Find three sensations.

Feet on the floor.

Hands on your legs.

Back against the chair.

Texture of your shirt.

Temperature of the air.

Pressure in your shoes.

Do not force emotion.

Start with sensation.

**Step three: Anchor to the room.**

Find five things you can see.

Three sounds you can hear.

One thing you can touch.

Say silently:

This is the room I am in now.

This is the date I am in now.

This is the body I am in now.

The goal is not to make dissociation disappear forever.

The goal is to practice return.

One sensation at a time.”

## Implementation Notes — NOT READ ALOUD

**For Facilitators:**

- Dissociation can increase while discussing dissociation. Keep eyes-open grounding available.
- Avoid long abstract explanations. Use concrete sensory language: feet, hands, room, sound, breath, object.
- Do not ask participants to describe traumatic memories.
- If someone appears far away, do not publicly call them out. Follow host-site support protocol quietly.
- Do not frame dissociation as weird, rare, or dramatic. Normalize it as protective distance.
- If someone reports losing time, unsafe driving, severe dissociation, or inability to stay safe, refer to licensed clinical or crisis support immediately according to host-site procedures.
- This session follows internal conflict because competing survival systems can become overwhelming enough that the mind creates distance.
- Session 14 moves into noncompliance because systems often misread dissociation and freeze as refusal, disrespect, or lack of effort.
- WRH is education, not treatment, diagnosis, medical advice, legal advice, or crisis care.
