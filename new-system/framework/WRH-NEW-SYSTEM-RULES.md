# WRH New System Rules

The new WRH system converts the old curriculum map into standardized word-for-word facilitator scripts.

## Core Identity

WRH is a standardized, non-clinical, no-disclosure psychoeducational curriculum.

It teaches survival responses, nervous-system adaptation, shame cycles, freeze responses, addiction patterns, avoidance, burnout, dissociation, grief, institutional misreading, and recovery language as understandable system patterns rather than character defects.

WRH does not function as therapy.

WRH does not diagnose participants.

WRH does not ask participants to disclose trauma.

WRH does not require participants to process personal history in front of a group.

The central teaching frame is:

> People cannot interrupt a pattern they have never been taught to see.

## Delivery Standard

Every WRH session must be written as a complete facilitator script.

The facilitator must be able to read the session word for word.

The facilitator is not expected to improvise, summarize, reinterpret, or make the lesson their own.

The curriculum must deliver the same core words in Little Rock, New York, Germany, or any other location.

## Standard Session Structure

Each session should include:

1. Facilitator Notes
2. Session Title
3. Plain-Language Teaching Title
4. Session Purpose
5. Participant Takeaway
6. Scope Boundary
7. Opening Script
8. Grounding / Regulation Moment
9. Core Teaching Section One
10. Core Teaching Section Two
11. Lived-Pattern Anchor from *From the Storm to the Fire*
12. Mechanism Map
13. Reflection Prompt
14. Practical Tool / Reframe
15. Integration Script
16. Closing Script
17. Cliffhanger into the next session
18. Implementation Notes

## Word-for-Word Rule

Every core teaching section must be written in spoken language.

Do not write loose prompts such as “Explain shame.”

Write the actual words the facilitator says.

## No-Disclosure Rule

No WRH session may require participants to disclose personal trauma history.

Participants may learn the pattern without telling their story.

Reflection prompts must allow silent participation.

Use language such as:

- You do not have to answer out loud.
- You can learn the pattern without telling your story.
- We are studying the mechanism, not forcing disclosure.

## Non-Clinical Boundary Rule

WRH is not therapy.

WRH is not medical advice.

WRH is not legal advice.

WRH is not diagnosis.

WRH is not treatment.

WRH is not crisis care.

WRH is structured psychoeducation.

## Mechanism-First Rule

WRH does not start with blame.

WRH starts with mechanism.

Every session should help participants understand:

- what the pattern is
- why the body may use it
- how it helped someone survive
- how it can become harmful later
- where an interruption point exists
- what replacement language or tool can be practiced

## Shame-Reduction Without Excuse Rule

WRH reduces shame without removing responsibility.

Understanding the pattern does not erase damage.

Explaining the mechanism does not mean nobody was hurt.

Accountability becomes more possible when shame stops running the room.

The balance is:

No shame.
No excuse.
More clarity.
More responsibility.
More interruption points.

## Lived-Anchor Rule

The sessions should be grounded in lived source material from *From the Storm to the Fire* when relevant.

The lived material should serve the teaching.

The question is always:

> What pattern does this event teach?

## Participant Safety Rule

Every session must assume that someone in the room may be activated, ashamed, grieving, dissociated, angry, shut down, or silently overwhelmed.

The facilitator must not corner participants, demand eye contact, call on people unexpectedly during vulnerable sections, or reward only verbal participation.

Silent learning counts.

## Repetition Rule

WRH repeats core truths across sessions because participants in survival mode often need repetition before a concept becomes usable under stress.

Key repeated phrases may include:

- This is not about blame. This is about pattern recognition.
- You cannot interrupt a pattern you have never been taught to see.
- Freeze is not refusal.
- Avoidance is not always defiance.
- Shame is not accountability.
- Relief is not recovery.
- Getting clean is not the same as getting free.
- Understanding is not an excuse. It is the beginning of responsibility.

## Plain-Language Translation Rule

Clinical or technical terms must be translated.

Examples:

- Autonomic dysregulation: the body’s alarm system is firing too high, too long, or at the wrong time.
- Executive dysfunction: the part of the brain that starts, organizes, remembers, and completes tasks is disrupted under stress.
- Dissociation: the system creates distance from the moment because staying fully present feels like too much.
- Freeze: the body hits the brakes when fight or flight feels impossible.

## Tone Rule

WRH should sound serious, steady, human, grounded, and direct.

It should not sound like motivational fluff, social-media therapy, generic wellness language, or a sermon.

## Case Study Rule

The case study must show the pattern, not merely retell biography.

The story serves the mechanism.

## Accountability Rule

Every session that explains survival behavior must also preserve accountability.

The correct takeaway is:

> My system learned patterns that helped me survive. Some of those patterns now hurt me or other people. If I can see them earlier, I have a better chance of interrupting them.

## Regulation Rule

Every session should include at least one regulation moment.

This may include breath, feet on floor, wall press, looking around the room, unclenching hands, naming neutral objects, or silent pause.

## Reflection Rule

Reflection prompts must be safe, optional, and non-disclosing.

They should build pattern recognition, not confession.

## Group Discussion Rule

The facilitator should not let the room turn into cross-talk, advice-giving, trauma dumping, diagnosing, debating, or rescuing.

The group studies mechanisms, not personal histories.

## Crisis Boundary Rule

Any session touching suicide, self-harm, overdose, domestic violence, severe withdrawal, psychosis, or immediate danger must include escalation language.

WRH can teach crisis patterns.

WRH cannot replace crisis care.

## Architecture Rule

The 76-session system remains organized as:

- Part I: The System Logic Map — Sessions 1–26
- Part II: The Core Operational Journey — Sessions 27–56
- Part III: Advanced System Specialization — Sessions 57–75
- Part IV: Facilitator Standardization Toolkit and Safety Addendum
- Session 76: Behavioral Health Crisis Response, Safety Protocols, and Escalation Management Framework

## Naming Rule

Session titles may remain formal in the repo index.

Inside the session, each title should also have a human-facing plain-language title.

## Fidelity Rule

The new WRH curriculum must be delivery-faithful:

- same core words
- same order
- same scope boundary
- same no-disclosure rule
- same mechanism-first approach
- same shame/accountability balance
- same closing integration

## Final Standard

A finished WRH session is complete only when:

- the facilitator can read it word for word
- the participant can understand it without clinical training
- the session does not require trauma disclosure
- the lesson explains mechanism before judgment
- the language reduces shame without erasing accountability
- the session includes regulation or reflection
- the boundary remains non-clinical
- the closing gives participants a usable sentence, tool, or reframe

The new WRH standard is not:

> Here is a topic. Teach it.

The new WRH standard is:

> Here are the words. Read them. Hold the boundary. Teach the pattern.
